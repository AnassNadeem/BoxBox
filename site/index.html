Methodology

Decision points. Rule-extracted from race timing data, never hand-picked: pit-stop neighborhoods (the laps around every real stop), safety-car and VSC deployments (top-10 cars), and undercut threats (a rival within 3.5s pits). Each point freezes exactly what a strategist knew entering that lap — nothing from the future leaks in, which is enforced by tests.

Scoring. A per-race simulator fits each car's tyre-degradation curve and pit loss from that race's own laps, then evaluates every candidate strategy (pit now on any compound / stay and stop later) for the focal car against two oracles. The headline Δ ex-ante compares the model to the best strategy choosable without knowing future safety cars — the same information the model has; 0 means matching a perfect ex-ante strategist, and negative is possible when a call happens to beat the ex-ante plan in the race as it actually unfolded. Δ hindsight compares to the retrospectively perfect strategy (knows every future SC/VSC) as context. Beat team %: how often the model's call beats what the real pit wall did. Flip rate: how often repeated identical prompts get different answers. Invalid %: responses that failed strict JSON parsing.

Contamination control. 2026 races post-date every model's training data. The same pipeline runs on 2024–25 races; a model scoring much better on old races than new ones is probably reciting, not reasoning.

Caveat. Both oracles share the simulator's fitted lap-time models; the ex-ante oracle assumes green-flag racing after the decision lap (it keeps the current lap's known track status, which models also see). All strategies are valued in the race as it really unfolded, so the ex-ante optimum is never faster than the hindsight optimum.
